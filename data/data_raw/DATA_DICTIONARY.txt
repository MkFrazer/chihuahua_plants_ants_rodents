This Data Dictionary explains the file formatting for the RAW DATA for the project, 
"Plant Species Diversity as a function of ant and rodent predation in the Chihuahua desert."

This study will only use data from 1989-2002, due to discrepencies in the plant data, as 
described below.
  
  
PLOT_TREATMENTS
  Plot treatments varied over the years. 
  
* plot_treatments.csv contained the qualitative version of the treatment table found with the 
  metadata on https://www.esapubs.org/archive/ecol/E090/118/metadata.htm. (see below for interpretation)
  
* treatments.csv contains a binary list for the presence (1) or absence (0) of each group, 
  interpreted from the qualitative version:
    LARGE_RODENTS = kangaroo rats
    ALL_RODENTS = all rodents
    PORU_ANTS = Pogonomyrmex rugosus, a dominant granivorous ant species
    ALL_ANTS = all ant colonies
  
  The metadata explains how exclusions were performed:
  
  Overview:
    "Experimental manipulations have been altered for some plots over the course of the 
    study. Changes in treatment assignment occurred in 1985 and 1987. Changes in treatment 
    were initiated when the manipulation did not cause significant changes in the rodent 
    community (see Brown 1998 for details). Because responses were either non-existent or 
    minor, plots that were plant manipulations or seed additions from 1977–1987 and were 
    assigned as controls in 1987 are often treated as “long-term controls” for research 
    focusing on the long-term dynamics at the site."
    
  Rodents:
    "Rodent manipulations are effected through the use of gates in the fencing of each plot. 
    Rodent removals contain no gates and any rodents captured on those plots are promptly 
    removed. All other plots contain 16 gates (4 per plot side); gates consist of holes cut 
    through the hardware cloth of the fencing. Gate size is manipulated to exclude subsets of 
    the rodent community. Dimensions for gates on kangaroo rat removal plots are 1.9 cm × 1.9 
    cm, D. spectabilis removals were 2.6 cm × 3.0 cm, and control plots are 3.7 cm × 5.7 cm. 
    Species caught on plots from which they are supposed to be excluded are removed from the 
    site and the access point to the plot is located and eliminated."
    
  Ants:
    "Since 1977, ant manipulations have been conducted by applying a commercial poison 
    (Mirex [Allied Chemical Corporation] through 1980 and AMDRO [American Cyanamide Company] 
    afterwards) to designated plots (Davidson et al 1985). Removal of all ants was conducted 
    by widespread application of the baited poison across the designated plot. Selected removal 
    of Pogonomyrmex rugosus was conducted by application of the baited poison at conspicuous 
    mounds of that species (Brown 1998)."
    
  Plant removal and seed additions:
    Plant treatments: "Since 1988 there have been no direct manipulations of the plant community. 
      Before 1988, annuals were “removed” by applying an herbicide (brand: Roundup), but this removal
      was not considered successful and was discontinued (Brown 1998)."
    Seed additions: "Since 1985 there have been no seed additions to any plot. Before 1985, seed 
      additions were conducted by applying 96 kg milo (Sorghum vulgare) and/or millet (Panicum miliaceum) 
      seeds to designated plots (Davidson et al 1985)."
      
PLANTS_RAW
  Each CSV file contains winter or summer annual or perennial plant abundances measured from 
  1983-2002. These files will be combined in the data_clean folder to calculate total abundance per plot 
  per year.
  Data Collection Period, Frequency: 
    "Plant data were collected continuously from 1978–2002." A variety of documented issues (Samson et al. 
    1992, Davidson et al. 1985, Quo et al. 2002) meant that 1978–1982 data were not published. 
    Additionally, "Before 1988, it is possible that abundances were not necessarily recorded for all species 
    present on a quadrat. Various assessments of the pre-1989 data have suggested that data may not have been 
    collected on the rare species (Samson et al. 1992, Davidson et al. 1985, Quo et al. 2002), but when the 
    transition to complete community sampling occurred is not clear. We are confident that complete community 
    sampling was occurring by 1989." 
    As such, this study only analyzes the years 1989-2002.
  Data Collection:
    "Because there are two annual plant communities – one in the winter and one in the summer - there are two 
    plant surveys per year. The surveys occur towards the end of the growing season; occurring in spring for 
    the winter community and fall for the summer community. Perennials occurring on quadrats were also recorded."
  Research Methods:
    Field: "Plants rooted within 16 fixed quadrats in each plot are counted each spring (winter annual survey) and 
    fall (summer annual survey). Quadrat dimensions: 0.25 × 0.25 m. Quadrats are placed at locations permanently 
    marked by a rebar stake." 
  Values:
    For each species abbreviation (see metadata website for species info),
    0 = no individuals present; -99 = presence/absence not recorded.

  Note: 
   This study investigates the total plant species abundance per plot per year, so species-level
   data is not relevant here. For more information about species-specific data, see the metadata 
   associated with the plant abundance data (https://www.esapubs.org/archive/ecol/E090/118/Portal_plant_metadata.htm)
  
  
PRECIP_RAW
  These CSV files contain precipitation data from 1980-2002. "Because the spatial scale of the study 
  site is relatively small relative to the scale at which precipitation events occur, there is only one
  precipitation measurement for the site. The location of the precipitation gauge can be found on the 
  map of the site ( metadata.htm; Fig 1). There are two precipitation data files (1980–1989 and 1989–2002). 
  The separate files reflect changes in the time scale at which precipitation data was collected 
  (approximately daily-to-weekly vs. hourly, respectively)." 
  For this study, we used average monthly precipitation per year (see data_processed).
  
  Note:
    "There are two rainy seasons at the site, occurring roughly from Oct–April and July–Sept. 
    Typically 60% of the annual rainfall occurs in the summer (Brown and Ernest 2002). The two 
    rainy seasons result in generally two distinct annual plant communities, with a few bi-seasonal 
    annual species." For more information related to precipitation collection, see 
    (https://www.esapubs.org/archive/ecol/E090/118/Portal_precipitation_metadata.htm).
